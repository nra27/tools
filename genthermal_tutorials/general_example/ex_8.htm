Exercise 8 - Absolute & Relative Total Temperatures

The concept of Absolute and Relative Total temperatures is an important one in defining the correct boundary conditions within SC03. 

Exercise Objectives

To gain practice in modelling in SC03 using Absolute and Relative Total temperatures.

Problem

The General Example model has been updated to include additional geometry for some of the static structure in front of the HP turbine. There are several boundary conditions omitted from the SC03 model provided, and this tutorial will guide the user through the various methods required to complete the definition of the boundary conditions. There are four main tasks which will cover the following modelling examples:

  • Task 1 - Using the ABS2REL and REL2ABS correlation to switch reference frames between static and rotating components

  • Task 2 - Modelling inter-disc cavities in the HP compressor in the Relative frame of reference

  • Task 3 - Modelling flow through a Labyrinth seal in the Absolute frame of reference, including windage heating

  • Task 4 - Modelling windage heating in 'rectangular' cavities in the Absolute frame of reference

 

Theory

Before beginning the tutorial, it is necessary to cover some of the theory behind Absolute and Relative Total Temperatures, and windage heating.  This theory is covered in detail on the Absolute & Relative Total temperatures page of the Thermo-mechanics web. Here we will revise some of the key aspects of this theory, for derivations and more details, please refer back to the Absolute & Relative Total temperatures page.

Absolute & Relative Total Temperatures

A Total temperature, T, is a measure of the total energy of a gas. There are two components to a Total temperature - a static temperature, t, and a velocity term. The static temperature of a gas is a measure of the internal energy of all of the molecules of the gas, moving in random directions - the traditional way of describing the molecular movements in a gas. The velocity term is a measure of the energy of the gas as a result of any bulk movements, for example, the velocity of the air blown in to a gas turbine by the Fan. In a gas turbine, the air is moving axially through the engine, and it is also moving around the circumference of the engine. This circumferential velocity is called the Swirl Velocity, Vf.


Now, considering only the Swirl velocity, imagine standing on a stationary component in the engine, with the air flowing circumferentially around the engine. You would feel the air moving past you at a velocity equal to Vf. This is called the Absolute velocity. Now, consider the same situation, but this time the component that you are standing on is rotating in the same direction as the air is flowing, such that wr =V1, and V1 is less than Vf. You would now feel the air moving past you at a velocity equal to the difference between the air velocity and the component velocity. This is called the Relative velocity; Vrel = Vf - V1. As the absolute and relative total temperatures can be quite different, it is important that when creating an SC03 model that has static and rotating components the correct temperature is used in the boundary conditions. There are two ways this can be done, and both will be covered later on in the tutorial.

Windage Heating

In a rotor - stator cavity, the bulk air swirl velocity is generally somewhere between 0 and disc speed. However, at the surface of the stator, the velocity is 0 (no slip condition). There is a velocity profile in the boundary layer as the velocity increases to the bulk air velocity. Viscous forces in this boundary layer cause the air to heat up. Similarly, at the surface of the rotor the air is moving at disc speed and there is a velocity profile in the boundary layer as the air velocity decreases to the bulk air velocity. Again, viscous forces in the boundary layer cause heating of the air. This viscous heating is known within Rolls Royce as Windage heating (the term windage is sometimes applied to different phenomena in text books).
