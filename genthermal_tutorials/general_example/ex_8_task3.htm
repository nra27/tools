Exercise 8 - Task 3: Windage Heating in Labyrinth Seals

Labyrinth seals are used extensively in Gas Turbine Engines. The metal surface area is relatively large, and the clearances are small, so the work done on the air as it passes through the seal can be large.

Instructions

As discussed in the Theory section, windage heating is the Rolls Royce term for the viscous heating that occurs as a result of shear forces in the fluid boundary layer. 

Labyrinth seals are very common in Gas Turbine engines. Figure 8 below shows an example of some labyrinth seals on the HP turbine of the Trent 1000 engine. Click here to see the Trent 900 air system diagram and look for other locations in the engine where labyrinth seals are used.

Figure 8 - Trent 1000 HP Turbine disc & static structure

A labyrinth seal consists of a number of fins with a small clearance to an abradable lining. Under certain engine conditions the fins may rub on the abradable lining. In order for the air to pass through the seal it has to navigate a circuitous path around each of the fins. As can be appreciated, this is a complex flow problem, so an empirical correlation is very useful in approximating the windage heating. This correlation is called LAB43231 and is used in the heat pickup field of a stream or a duct. It takes information about the flow through the seal, and the seal geometry to calculate the work done on the air, and thus the heat input to the air. It can also be used to calculate the change in swirl velocity through the seal. This functionality will be describe din more detail later on.

The General Example model has been updated to include some geometry similar to that shown in Figure 8 above. There are now 4 separate labyrinth seals between the HP turbine disc, and the static structure in front of it. Figure 9  below shows these 4 seals:

Figure 9 - Labyrinth seals in General Example model

This geometry is a simplified version of the Trent 100 design shown in Figure 8. To investigate the modelling of one of these seals, will will look at the inner seal on the disc. 

When we modelled the flow on the HP compressor group, we worked in the relative frame of reference. That is, we converted all of the inputs into the relative frame of reference, so that SC03 did not have to do any of the conversions itself. In the Turbines thermals group, it is convention to model the flow up the disc face in the Absolute frame of reference. This means that all of the inputs need to be in the Absolute frame of reference. This is fine for any flows on the static structure - they are already in the Absolute frame of reference. However, the flows on the rotating components are in the relative frame of reference, and need to be converted to Absolute temperatures.

The turbines group also models the flow through a labyrinth seal using a combination of streams and ducts, with the assumption that the abradable lining is semi-permeable. The Duct boundary condition is applied to the metal surface on the rotating component, and the metal surface beneath the abradable lining on the static component 95% of the total mass flow through the seal is through the duct. The remaining 5% of the total mass flow is applied through a stream that flows over the outer surface of the abradable lining. This is shown for the first seal in Figure 10 below:

Figure 10 - Boundary conditions applied to first labyrinth seal

The total flow through the first labyrinth seal is equal to the mass flow of stream 24 which flows long the HP shaft and up the turbine disc plus the mass flow coming down the static structure (stream 504). The mix of these two streams flows through the seal. In order to calculate the temperature of the flow into the seal, the MIX function is used. In simplistic terms, this gives a mass flow weighted average temperature of the two streams. To use the MIX function, both temperatures must be in the same frame of reference - unfortunately the flow from the static structure is in the absolute frame, whilst the flow up the turbine disc is in the relative frame.

The ABS2REL functionality cannot be used inside the MIX function. Instead, the conversion from a relative to an absolute temperature is carried out using a dummy stream. Stream 824 has the same mass flow as stream 24, and the temperature field contains the REL2ABS function which converts the relative total temperature of stream 24 into an absolute total temperature. All other fields in the boundary condition definition panel are set to 0. As there is no heat pickup and no heat transfer, the stream exit temperature will be the same as the stream inlet temperature, which is the absolute total temperature at the exit of stream 24. The exit temperature of stream 824 can now be referenced in the absolute frame of reference instead of the exit temperature of stream 24.

The input panel for duct 1 is shown below in Figure 11. Mass flow rates through a seal are usually defined by the Air Systems group and defined in the model either in the cycle definition, or in the user parameters as a scaling expression. In this case the mass flow rate through the seal is defined in the cycle definition as W2L101, and in line with the Turbines group convention, 0.95% of this mass flow goes through the duct. To ensure mass is conserved, the mass flow through the seal should be equal to the mass flow down the static structure plus the mass flow entrained up the turbine disc.

The inlet temperature is defined using the MIX( ) function, where stream 824 is a dummy stream that calculates the absolute temperature of stream 24 and stream 504 is the flow down the static structure. Similar to the PWR( ) function used in voids, preceding the index number with a '-' tells SC03 to use the temperature from a duct in the MIX calculations.

Figure 11 - Duct 1 input panel

The LAB43231 correlation is used in the heat pickup field for side A of the duct. It is convention in the Thermals group to apply side A of a duct to the rotating component, and side B to the static structure. Note that there is no heat pickup on the static structure side of the duct. The LAB43231 correlation requires 7 input parameters to perform its calculations:

LAB43231(Tabs in, wrotor, wstator, mass flow, inlet swirl velocity, area for heat pickup, geometry index)

All of these inputs are fairly self explanatory apart from the area and the geometry index. The mass flow specified should be the total mass flow that is subject to the heat input. This should be the total flow through the seal, not just the 95% that flows through the duct. As can be seen from the panel, the heat pickup is required in mW/mm2. In order to calculate this SC03 needs the surface area over which the work is done. The area over which work is done is only that of the rotating component - side A of the duct. In SC03 version 10 it is possible to reference the area of a boundary condition in much the same way as the temperature or pressure of a boundary condition is referenced. In this seal, the area required is that of side A of duct 1. The expression to reference this area is DA1a (D for duct, A for Area, 1a for side A of duct 1). 

The correlation requires some information about the geometry of the seal to calculate the work done on the air as it passes through. In order to simplify the input process, this geometry information is contained in a separate panel which is then referenced by the LAB43231 correlation. As this is the first time the correlation has been use din the model, the correlation references geometry panel 1. From SC03, the geometry input panel is referenced from the Thermals menu, then LAB43231. The geometry input panel for this seal is shown below in Figure 12:

Figure 12 - general example labyrinth seal 1 geometry input panel

The geometry input panel has a number of check boxes, and a number of data fields. The check boxes are self explanatory, the number of fins per step is only used on a stepped seal like those shown in the Trent 1000 example in Figure 3. Rotor inlet diameter & rotor exit diameter are easy to obtain using the Query Geometry tool (Model>Query Geometry) and clicking on the inlet and exit of the seal.

The radial clearance field refers to the seal running clearance. This is literally the size of the gap between the seal fin tips and the stator. In reality, this value varies through the engine cycle as the components undergo thermal expansion and the CF forces act on the rotating components, but the LAB43231 correlation requires a single value to be used. The value specified in this field should be consistent with the value used in the SPAN analysis (SPAN is a 1D flow analysis programme that calculates mass flows and pressures throughout the engine at a steady-state condition). For this tutorial, use a value of 0.140mm for all seals.

Axial clearance is only required for a stepped seal. This is the axial clearance between the fin tips and the edge of the step. Fin pitch is the distance between the Fin tips (front of one fin tip to the front of the next fin tip). Fin tip thickness can again be found using the Query Geometry tool and zooming in on the end of a fin. Honeycomb cell size refers to the abradable lining, and should be left as the default unless otherwise specified, as should the mass flow factor, and the two moment factors. The geometry panel should be defined before the boundary condition is applied to the model, otherwise SC03 will reject the expressions referencing a geometry panel that does not exist.

Going back to the duct input panel in Figure 11 it is necessary to define the pressure through the seal. It is normal for this to be defined as the average of the pressure either side of seal. The heat transfer coefficient used is FRD - a turbulent free disc. In this case a factor of 0.6 is applied to the correlation which is normally determined by matching the model to experimental data. There is an additional factor on the HTC for side B of 0.5. This is because side B of the duct is applied to an internal line. SC03 sees an internal line as having two sides, and calculates the area accordingly. When both domains on either side of the internal line are axi-symmetric, or as in this case, one domain is defined as having a thickness of 2pr, the area SC03 uses in its calculations is double the actual area, so applying a factor of 0.5 to the HTC corrects it. When one, or both, of the domains is defined as having a thickness less than 2pr it is necessary to calculate the area SC03 will use and the actual area over which heat transfer occurs to calculate the correction factor. This scenario commonly occurs when modelling flow through a hole. How to model flow through holes, and the method used to calculate the factors is the subject of an SC03 Best Practice that can be found on the Thermo-mechanics web. There are some specific heat transfer correlations for labyrinth seals (LABTHR, LABTHS, LABSTPR, LABSTPS) but these are not commonly used.

The swirl velocity is defined in this case as SWIRL1. The SV43231 correlation can be used to calculate the swirl velocity at the exit of the labyrinth seal. This correlation uses exactly the same calculations as the LAB43231 correlation but outputs the swirl velocity instead of the heat pickup. It requires all of the input parameters as the LAB43231 correlation apart from the area. The user expression SWIRL1 is set up to vary the swirl velocity between the inlet and the exit of the seal. In SC03, the parametric length of any boundary condition is described by the variable 'S'. This variable has a value of zero at the start of the boundary condition, and 1 at the exit of the boundary condition, and varies linearly between the two. The SWIRL1 expression is shown below:

SWIRL1 = SV24 + S*(SV43231(MIX(824,504),NH,0,W2L101,SV24,1)-SV24)

This expression means that at the inlet to the duct, 'S' is zero, so the swirl velocity is just SV24 (the swirl velocity at the exit of stream 24) and at the exit of the duct when 'S' = 1 the swirl velocity is equal to the output of the SV43231 expression. An easy way to see the full expression in SC03 is to click in the Swirl Velocity field of the input panel (or Query BC's panel) and press 'Ctrl + E' on the keyboard. This will expand any expression in the field and display the results in the Monitor window. This will work in any panel field - try it in the Pressure field to see the scaling expression for P2C10.

Stream 25 flows over the outer surface of the abradable liner with 5% of the total mass flow. The input panel for stream 25 is shown below in Figure 13. The inlet temperature, pressure and the heat transfer coefficient are exactly the same as for the duct. There is no heat pickup or swirl velocity on the stream.

Look at the input panels for the duct and stream on seal 2 and seal 3 and satisfy yourself that the method used is the same as that used on the first labyrinth seal.

Use this method to define the boundary conditions for the final labyrinth seal (use duct 9 and stream 50 to maintain interdependencies for subsequent boundary conditions)

  • Use the Query Geometry tool to extract the data required for the seal geometry panel

  • Mass flow through the seal is defined as W2L104

  • Pressure upstream of the seal is defined in the user parameters as P1C12, and the downstream pressure is P1C06

  • Select an appropriate heat transfer correlation 

  • Define user parameter SWIRL4 to calculate the swirl velocity through the seal
