Exercise 8 - Task 2: Inter-disc Cavities

Now that the flows on the HP compressor discs are modelled in the Relative frame of reference, the inter-disc cavities can be modelled.

Instructions

In the Theory section on Windage Heating, Equation 7 describes the steady state flow equation in the relative frame of reference.

Equation 7

A key part of modelling inter-disc cavities is to ensure the Relative Total temperature increases with radius, as described above. Now, in an inter-disc cavity, the air is generally moving at disc speed, so there is no net drag - D is zero. It is also valid to assume there is no net heat input to the cavity, so Q is also zero. This means that the Absolute Total temperature must also rise as radius increases.

The standard way of modelling an inter-disc cavity is to use a void. Unfortunately, a void boundary condition has a uniform temperature throughout, so it cannot support the required variation in Absolute Total temperature. In order to overcome this problem, it is necessary to trick SC03 into giving the void a temperature that varies with radius.

Both the Absolute and Relative total temperature in the void is increasing with radius, related by swirl velocity. A simplification can be made by assuming the air in the cavity behaves like a free vortex i.e. r1v1=r2v2 - swirl ratio is constant throughout the cavity. This assumption means that a swirl velocity can be specified in terms of a single swirl ratio for the void boundary condition, so that although the input temperature is already in the Relative frame of reference, SC03 will calculate an equivalent temperature that varies with radius. Figure 4 below shows a diagram of an inter-disc cavity.

Figure 4 - Diagram of Inter-disc cavity

The swirl velocity must be defined as a function of radius, so that the temperature will increase with radius. The expression used is shown below in Equation 8:

Equation 8

In this expression, r1 is the inner radius of the void and must be specified as a number. In most inter-disc cavities, the void will be asymmetric, as shown above in Figure 4. For simplicity use the average inner radius. There are more sophisticated techniques for accounting for the difference in radius detailed on the Inter-disc cavities Standard Feature web page. When the radius is equal to the inner radius, i.e. at the void inlet r1/r = 1, the swirl velocity is simply half disc speed. As mentioned previously in the Theory section, when the swirl velocity is half disc speed, the Absolute and Relative total temperatures are the same, so SC03 will not convert the temperature. As the radius r increases, the swirl velocity defined by Equation 8 will decrease, so SC03 will perform a conversion on the inlet temperature based on the relationship between Absolute and Relative Total temperature, assuming the inlet temperature is an Absolute Total temperature (don't forget it isn't! We are tricking SC03 into making a conversion!). This relationship shows that as the swirl velocity decreases below half disc speed, the Relative Total temperature becomes larger than the Absolute Total temperature, and thus we have our temperature rise with radius.

The general example model has two inter-disc cavities on the HP compressor. These are modelled as Void 10 and Void 1, shown below in Figure 5:

You may notice that the voids are defined in a slightly different way to the previous general examples - each void now has three separate sub-indices e.g. Void 10.1, Void 10.2 and Void 10.3. The reason for this will become apparent soon.

The input panel for Void 10.1 is shown below in Figure 6.The power input field contains the expression PWR(3). This tells SC03 that the stream 3 flows into the void, and the power from this stream raises the bulk temperature in the void. where DT is simply the stream temperature minus the void temperature. Power inputs can come from a stream or a duct. If the power input comes from a duct, the same PWR( ) expression is used, but the duct index is preceded by a '-'. For example, the expression PWR(25) tells SC03 that the void has a power input from stream 25, and PWR(-4) indicates a power input from Duct 4. The '-' does not mean that the power has a negative value. It should be noted that only one boundary condition can be specified in the PWR( ) expression. If there are multiple power inputs, simply add expressions together, for example: PWR(25) + PWR(-4) would mean there is a power input from both stream 25 and duct 4.

Figure 6 - Void 10.1 input panel

The pressure of the boundary condition is consistent with the inlet - stream 3 (this could also be defined using interdependencies as SP3, the pressure of stream 3), and the NVP heat transfer correlation is used. This is the correlation for natural convection from a vertical plate, which is a fairly good approximation for the vertical disc we have in reality. The only parameter required is the plate length, which is usually defined as the outer radius of the disc.

The swirl velocity is defined in the same form as Equation 8, where the inner radius of the void is approximately 195mm.

The cavity is modelled in three sections - void 10.1, void 10.2, and void 10.3. As all three voids have the same main index (10), they have the same power input and pressures, and by definition, the same temperature. Splitting the void into sub-voids (.1, .2, and .3) means that different heat transfer correlations can be used. Void 10.3 is very similar to void 10.1 in that it is a spinning disc that can be approximated to a vertical plate, so it is usual to use the same heat transfer coefficient for each side of the cavity. It should be noted that a matching exercise may apply different factors to each side of the cavity.

Void 10.2 is not the same as the sides of cavity. It is effectively the inside surface of a large diameter cylinder. It is normal to assume that the diameter is so large (curvature of surface is low) that it can be approximated to a flat horizontal plate. SC03 has separate correlations for the upper and lower surface of a flat plate, and it is normal to assume, looking at the geometry, that void 10.2 is the lower surface of a plate. However, a rotating system, such as a gas turbine, behaves slightly differently to this.

In normal convection from a hot plate, the air local to the plate heats up, becomes less dense, and rises away from the plate, against gravity. In a gas turbine engine, the air is spinning round at high speeds, and centrifugal (or centripetal) forces come into play, pushing the heavy, cold air to the outside of the engine, which leaves the hot air from the plate to move towards the centre of the engine, or downwards in a 2D axi-symmetric model. An arrow on the engine diagram to show the typical direction of gravity will point downwards, and drawing an arrow on the same diagram to show the direction of the centrifugal force will point outwards - in a 2D axi-symmetric model, gravity is effectively reversed. This means that the most suitable correlation to use for void 10.2 is NUS - natural convection from the upper surface of a flat plate. The input panel for void 10.2 is shown below:

The power input, pressure, and swirl velocity are all the same as for void 10.1. The only difference is in the heat transfer correlation used. The NUS correlation only requires one input parameter - the hydraulic diameter. The hydraulic diameter is equal to four times the area of the boundary condition, divided by the perimeter. The area is equal to the axial length of the boundary condition (i.e. gap between discs), multiplied by the circumference (2pr). The perimeter is equal to two times the circumference plus two times the axial length.
